The Home of Walking Safari

South Luangwa. Where
the walking safari was born.

Zambia is where the walking safari was invented — in the South Luangwa Valley, in the early 1950s, when Norman Carr led the first guests out of camp and into the bush on foot. The practice has never left, and the guides it has produced are among the finest in Africa. Walking through the Luangwa Valley with a Zambian professional guide — reading tracks, interpreting sound and smell, understanding the relationships between species and the logic of the landscape — is a form of safari that no vehicle-based experience can fully replicate.

The South Luangwa Valley, flanked by the Muchinga Escarpment and drained by the Luangwa River, holds one of the highest concentrations of wildlife in Africa during the dry season. Leopard here are habituated to vehicles and frequently observed at close range. Wild dog, lion, elephant, hippo, and a rich diversity of birds populate the valley floor. To the south, the Lower Zambezi National Park offers a complementary experience: canoe safaris along the Zambezi, where the absence of engine noise allows an intimacy with wildlife that no conventional game drive provides.

Walking Safari

South Luangwa National Park

South Luangwa is the undisputed home of the walking safari — a 9,050-square-kilometre national park in eastern Zambia whose professional guides are trained under one of Africa's most rigorous qualification systems. The park's wildlife — elephant, lion, leopard, wild dog, hippo, crocodile — concentrates dramatically along the Luangwa River in the dry season, when the receding water creates a corridor of extraordinary activity. Night game drives, permitted in the private concessions on the park's boundary, reveal a completely different face of the bush. The camps here — small, owner-managed, deeply connected to the landscape — are among the finest in Africa.

Canoe Safari

Lower Zambezi National Park

The Lower Zambezi National Park occupies the valley floor between the Zambezi escarpment and the river itself — a landscape of riverine forest, open floodplain, and mopane woodland that supports exceptional wildlife and some of Africa's finest canoeing. Multi-day canoe safaris along the Zambezi, staying in fly camps on the riverbank, offer a quality of immersion that no other activity in Africa matches: drifting past elephant at the water's edge at eye level, passing sandbanks occupied by crocodile, watching hippo surface alongside the canoe. The Zambezi's tigerfish are also among the most prized freshwater sport fish in the world — Zambia is the premier destination for fly fishing for them.

Night Safari

Night Drives & Leopard

South Luangwa has one of the highest leopard densities in Africa and is one of the best places in the world to observe them. The leopards of the private concessions adjacent to the national park have been observed regularly over many years and show little concern for vehicles — encounters are frequently extended and observed at very close range. Night game drives in the concessions, where the rules of the national park do not apply, reveal porcupine, civet, serval, and the nocturnal behaviour of predators and prey that no amount of day game driving can provide. The combination of walking by day and night drives after dark makes Luangwa uniquely complete.

Wilderness

Remote Zambia & the Kafue

Beyond the Luangwa and Lower Zambezi, Zambia offers vast tracts of protected wilderness that see very few visitors. Kafue National Park, at over 22,000 square kilometres, is one of the largest national parks in Africa and shelters exceptional wildlife including the rarely seen sitatunga, red lechwe, and wild dog. The Bangweulu Wetlands, in the north, are among the most important wetland systems in Africa — home to the shoebill stork, one of the world's most extraordinary birds. Zambia's scale and commitment to conservation creates a country where genuinely remote, unvisited safari remains possible for those who seek it.
