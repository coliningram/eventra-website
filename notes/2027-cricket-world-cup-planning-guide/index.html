The 2027 Cricket World Cup in South Africa: An Honest Planning Guide

Published 2 June 2026 5 minute read

The 2027 ICC Men's Cricket World Cup will be played across South Africa, Zimbabwe and Namibia in October and November 2027. It is the first time the tournament has returned to the African continent since 2003, contested by an expanded field of 14 nations in the 50 over one day format.

If you are thinking about being there, the short version is this. Decide early, because the best lodges and hotels sell out long before tickets become the constraint, and the timing of the tournament makes a combined cricket and safari trip genuinely workable if you plan it the right way.

Here is what actually matters.

When and where is it?

The tournament runs across October and November 2027, and it is a 54 match event. South Africa hosts 44 of those matches, with Zimbabwe and Namibia sharing the other 10 between them.

In South Africa, eight host cities have already been named: Johannesburg, Pretoria, Cape Town, Durban, Gqeberha, Bloemfontein, East London and Paarl. Zimbabwe and Namibia take their share around Harare, Bulawayo and Windhoek. What has not yet been published is the detailed fixture schedule, meaning which team plays where and on what date, and that is expected later in 2026. So the host window of October to November is fixed, but exactly which match lands in which city is still to come. That gap matters for planning, for reasons I come to under safari timing.

Match ticket, hospitality package, or private suite. What is the difference?

These are three different things and people conflate them constantly.

A match ticket is a seat. You sit in the public stands and arrange everything else yourself.

A hospitality package adds a premium space, food and drink, and usually a better view, sold per person. It is the middle option, and it is what most corporate guests picture when they say hospitality.

A private suite is an enclosed box for your own group, booked exclusively, with catering and dedicated service. It suits a company hosting clients or a family who want their own space for the day. It carries the highest cost per head and the least availability, so it is the first thing to disappear.

Which one is right depends entirely on who you are bringing and why. A group of friends wants tickets and a good bar nearby. A company entertaining clients wants a suite. Most people do not need the most expensive option, and a good operator should tell you that rather than upsell you.

When should you book?

Earlier than you think, and here is the honest reason.

Ticket sales for an event like this open through official ballots and on sale dates that will be announced through 2026 and 2027. But tickets are rarely the bottleneck. The bottleneck is everything around the cricket. The strong safari lodges and the best hotels in Cape Town and the Winelands hold limited rooms, and they fill for the October to November window well over a year out, regardless of the cricket. Flights to the region typically become bookable around a year ahead, so late 2026 onwards. If your trip depends on a particular lodge or a specific hotel, you are really booking against lodge availability, not ticket availability.

Can you combine the cricket with a safari?

Yes, and the timing of this tournament suits it unusually well, which is worth understanding rather than taking on faith.

South Africa's safari regions and its Cape coast run on opposite seasons. The bushveld, meaning Kruger and the private reserves, is at its best for game viewing in the dry winter and into October, when thinning vegetation and shrinking waterholes concentrate the wildlife and make sightings far more reliable. The Western Cape, meaning Cape Town, the Winelands and the coast, comes into its own from late spring through summer, roughly November to March.

October and November sit right on the hinge between the two. Early in the tournament, in October, the bushveld is still in prime dry season form, so a safari leg before or after a match works beautifully. As the tournament moves into November, the northern reserves begin to catch the first summer rains while Cape Town warms into its best months, so the balance shifts toward the coast and the Winelands.

In practical terms, an October trip leans naturally toward bush then cricket, and a November trip leans toward cricket then Cape. Knowing which way to tilt is the difference between a trip that flows and one that fights the seasons.

How the three host countries change the logistics

A tournament spread across South Africa, Zimbabwe and Namibia is not a single destination. Internal flights, border crossings, and very different infrastructure between the three mean that following one team across the group stage is a real logistical exercise, not a taxi ride.

For most visitors the sensible approach is to base around a cluster of matches in one country, usually South Africa, and treat any cross border leg as a deliberate, separately planned extension rather than an afterthought. Victoria Falls, on the Zimbabwe side, is the obvious and worthwhile one.

What we would tell you honestly

Three things most marketing pages will not say.

You do not need to attend every match. Trying to will exhaust you and your budget. Pick the fixtures that matter to you and build a proper trip around them.

The most expensive hospitality option is not always the right one. Match it to who you are bringing.

And the safari is often the part guests remember longest, not the cricket. Give it the time it deserves rather than squeezing it into a spare morning.

If you are starting to think about the 2027 Cricket World Cup, the useful thing to do now is not to buy anything. It is to decide roughly when in the October to November window you want to be there, and what kind of trip you actually want around the cricket. Everything else follows from that.
